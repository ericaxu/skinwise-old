  • Mandelic Acid

    Exfoliant Melanin Inhibitor

    Mandelic acid is one of the largest alpha hydroxy acids (AHAs), and derived from bitter almonds. In other areas of healthcare it is used as an antibacterial, but for skincare, it's useful for treating irregular pigmentation, acne, photoaging and mature dry skin.

  • Algae Extract

    Anti-inflammatory Emollient

    Algae is a water-dwelling plant packed with carotenoids, a group of plant antioxidants that promote healthy cell behavior. This antioxidant goo is extracted from algae with the hopes that those benefits will carry over to a topical product.

  • Lemon Juice

    Irritant

    Lemon juice is possibly the most common ingredient in DIY recipes - not just for skincare, but also cleaning, haircare and of course, food. It's touted to be lightening, anti-acne, and exfoliating. While it does contain chemicals that do those things (vitamin C, niacin, citric acid), it also comes with a dangerous catch.

  • Alpha Lipoic Acid

    Antioxidant

    Alpha-lipoic acid (ALA) is an antioxidant, similar to vitamins A, C and E, and ubiquinone (coenzyme Q10). It is found in very limited amounts in healthy skin cells, speeds up wound healing, and helps protect the skin against damage caused by exposure to UVB rays.

  • Azelaic Acid

    Melanin Inhibitor

    Azelaic acid is a naturally-occurring dicarboxylic acid that is an effective external treatment for acne, rosacea, melasma, and postinflammatory hyperpigmentation.

  • Benzoyl Peroxide

    Antibacterial

    Benzoyl peroxide is an extremely popular acne treatment that is cheap, safe, and very effective. It functions primarily as an antimicrobial agent, obliterating bacteria such as the acne-causing Propionibacterium acnes on the surface skin.

  • Glycerin

    Moisturizer

    Glycerin (also called glycerol) is a very effective humectant, used in many moisturizers to increase skin hydration, and in a lot of other important products as well (it can be used as a laxative, and to make explosive nitroglycerin). It occurs naturally in your skin as part of its home-grown moisturizing system, the natural moisturising factor (NMF), but the glycerin in your cream comes from fats and oils during the production of soap and biodiesel - each molecule of fat or oil breaks up to give one molecule of glycerin.
